Trends in Developer Jobs: A Meta Analysis of Stack Overflow Surveys

I‚Äôm really interested in the trends we see in the software engineering job market. Sometimes it‚Äôs really hard to tell a cohesive and accurate narrative about what‚Äôs happening because it just happens so dang fast, and very few people are collecting data on the matter.

For example, here are some questions that I‚Äôd like to know the answer to:

  • Does ‚ÄúDevOps‚Äù and increased cloud usage mean fewer traditional ops jobs?
  • Do things like Firebase and Supabase mean fewer back-end jobs?
  • Do things like tailwind mean fewer designers?
  • Are we moving into more specialist or more generalist roles?
  • Are people still specializing as DBAs?

I aggregated all the answers about developer employment from the last 9 years of Stack Overflow surveys to start being able to answer some of these questions. If you‚Äôre curious about the job outlook for developers, hopefully this is helpful data.

First, a note on the data üîó

  1. All the numbers in the tables represent the percentage of survey takers who identified themselves as a given job type.
  2. The questions asked by SO have changed over the years, so we should take that as a giant grain of salt.
  3. I‚Äôve normalized the answers. For example, ‚Äúdeveloper, back-end‚Äù and ‚Äúback-end web developer‚Äù I‚Äôve grouped together as ‚Äúbackend‚Äù.
  4. Stack overflow allowed people to select 0-many job titles after 2015, before that, it appears they were limited to one.
  5. In some years developers had more job options to pick from.
  6. I‚Äôve thrown away a lot of off-topic job types like ‚Äúenterprise services developer‚Äù and ‚Äúelected official‚Äù.
  7. This is only Stack Overflow data, so it‚Äôs biased by the size of their user base each year.

That said, let‚Äôs get into the data. I‚Äôll drop the script I wrote to create the aggregate data as well as a link to the raw data at the bottom of this article.

In 2013 the ratio of ‚Äúfront-end‚Äù developers to ‚Äúfull stack‚Äù developers was 15/85, while the ratio of ‚Äúback-end‚Äù developers to ‚Äúfull stack‚Äù developers was 24/76.

The trend of ‚Äúmore full stack, less specialization‚Äù continues through the entire ten years of data. It‚Äôs important to note that in 2017/2018 the survey changed drastically, which is why we see a big change in the numbers.

Interpretation #1 üîó

It seems reasonable to conclude that the trend for the last decade has been that there are a shrinking percentage of developers who do only front-end work or only back-end work. More and more are doubling as full-stack engineers. Keep in mind, this also might be that there have been more small to mid-sized companies over the years. Smaller companies typically require more generalists, but now I‚Äôm just guessing.

Interpretation #2 üîó

The ratio of front-end/back-end engineers has stayed fairly consistent, with there being slightly less than twice as many back-end engineers than front-end engineers.

This actually surprised me, I expected the ratio of front-end to back-end engineers to be closer throughout the years.

Caveat on the data for 2017 üîó

What the hell happened in 2017? The data honestly just seems broken. I dug through the data manually because according to the website, they claim 24% of web developers said they were back end, and 75% of respondents claimed to be web developers. At the moment it‚Äôs looking to me like they must have had some qualifiers on those numbers, because it‚Äôs just not adding up on my end. I‚Äôm going to exclude 2017 from my analysis in the interpretations.

Interpretation 1 üîó

DevOps appears to be gaining ground on traditional ops. In 2013 no one was identifying themselves as a ‚Äúdevops‚Äù person, but by 2020 and 2021 the numbers are looking very similar. It‚Äôs worth pointing out that in 2016 the devops numbers actually eclipsed the ‚Äúops‚Äù numbers for a single year. My best guess is that 2016 was about when many companies started simply rebranding their ‚Äúops‚Äù teams as ‚Äúdevops‚Äù teams to look cool.

It‚Äôs hard to care too much about these numbers, because devops is mostly being done wrong in my opinion. I don‚Äôt trust that the ‚Äúops‚Äù titles and the ‚Äúdevops‚Äù titles are all that different at most companies.

Interpretation 2 üîó

‚ÄúDevops‚Äù seems to be trending down the least in recent years, in fact it had a nice jump in 2022. However, if you look at ‚Äúdevops‚Äù and ‚Äúops‚Äù together, then the category is still trending down a bit. Interestingly ‚Äúops‚Äù has been trending down from the start, while ‚Äúback-end‚Äù was trending up until 2016 when the trend reversed and it‚Äôs been down since.

At first, I assumed we‚Äôre simply seeing the same trend that we saw in web development: more generalists, less specialists. However, I grew skeptical because when I looked across all job categories, I noticed nearly all of them were trending down‚Ä¶ which clearly can not be the case when we‚Äôre looking at percentages - it‚Äôs a zero sum game. I decided to add a new section to my script to dig in further. I calculated how many jobs on average each survey taker was laying claim to, and got this data:

It appears that from 2013-2015, developers were restricted to only submitting a single answer, which helps to account for the super low numbers. However, from 2019->2021 the average number of jobs per user went down, which is antithetical to the ‚Äúmore generalists‚Äù theory. It‚Äôs also worth pointing out, that as the years went on, Stack OVerflow actually added more specialized categories, which I then took the liberty of grouping into these broader groups. So there‚Äôs actually good evidence that developers are specializing more, or at the very least that there are more possible ways in which one can specialize.

That said, even after looking at this data I think there is still a good case to be made that back-end developers will be doing more and more ‚Äúdevops‚Äù work, especially at smaller companies.

My Script üîó

Here‚Äôs the full Python script I used to crunch the numbers. Sorry for the code sloppiness, I didn‚Äôt sink a ton of time into the code. The most interesting part of the script is probably the get_mapped_job function near the bottom, that‚Äôs where I aggregate all of the many job types reported by stack overflow users into the few I included in the chart.

def main():
    files = [

        (2018, [9]),
        (2019, [12]),
        (2020, [13]),
        (2021, [11]),
        (2022, [11]),
    ]

    out_dict = {}
    jobs_per_user_dict = {}

    for f_tup in files:
        counts = {}
        path = f"csv/{f_tup[0]}.csv"
        print(f"generating report for {path}")
        out_dict[f_tup[0]]: {}
        with open(path, "r") as csvfile:
            rows = csv.reader(csvfile, delimiter=",")
            count = 0
            rows_cpy = []
            jobs_per_user = []
            for row in rows:
                count += 1
                rows_cpy.append(row)
            for row in rows_cpy:
                try:
                    jobs = get_jobtext_from_cells(f_tup[1], row)

                    mapped_jobs = set()
                    for job in jobs:
                        mapped_jobs.add(get_mapped_job(job))
                    jobs_per_user.append(mapped_jobs)
                    for mapped_job in mapped_jobs:
                        if mapped_job not in counts:
                            counts[mapped_job] = 0
                        counts[mapped_job] += 1
                except Exception as e:
                    print(e)

            avg_jobs_per_user = 0
            for user_jobs in jobs_per_user:
                avg_jobs_per_user += len(user_jobs)
            jobs_per_user_dict[f_tup[0]] = round(
                avg_jobs_per_user / len(jobs_per_user), 2
            )

            for job in counts:
                counts[job] /= count
                counts[job] *= 100
                counts[job] = round(counts[job], 2)
            out_dict[f_tup[0]] = counts

    write_out(out_dict, jobs_per_user_dict)


def get_jobtext_from_cells(indexes, row):
    if len(indexes) == 0:
        return []
    job_texts = []
    for i in indexes:
        cell = row[i]
        cell_job_texts = cell.split(";")
        job_texts += cell_job_texts
    return job_texts


def write_out(out_dict, jobs_per_user_dict):
    types = [
        type_fullstack,
        type_frontend,
        type_backend,
        type_devops,
        type_ops,
        type_mobile,
        type_desktop,
        type_embedded,
        type_data_science,
        type_data_engineer,
        type_game,
        type_management,
        type_qa,
        type_education,
        type_design,
        type_analyst,
        type_marketer,
        type_ignore,
    ]

    with open(outpath, "w") as csvfile:
        w = csv.writer(csvfile)
        w.writerow(["year", "avg_jobs_per_user"] + types)
        for year in out_dict:
            row = [year, jobs_per_user_dict[year]]
            for t in types:
                row.append(out_dict[year][t] if t in out_dict[year] else 0)
            w.writerow(row)
